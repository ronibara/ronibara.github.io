Uber: Rider Research

Reimagining how we can move better.

Internship | 2022 | 14 weeks


What did I do at Uber?*

At Uber, I had the privilege to conduct evaluative and generative research on the rider trip experience. The TripEx team owns the experience from the moment you request your ride to the moment you step out of the vehicle and rate & tip.

*This work is subject to a Non-Disclosure Agreement, so I cannot share any further information than what’s on this page; nevertheless, I would love to chat and share the high-level UXR lessons that I learned along the way!

Project 1 Overview

For my first project, I led 12 usability sessions to assess the app’s information architecture during a rider’s trip experience. From these sessions, I drew 9 foundational insights around how riders prioritize trip information during each phase of the trip experience. Following these insights, I developed 6 actionable design recommendations to wrap up my report deck; in the 4 weeks following my share-out, 41 stakeholders visited my deck.

Personally, it was really cool to see this continued interest, but what makes me even happier is seeing my 6 recommendations​​ implemented into the 2023 H1 TripEx roadmap. In the coming year, I can’t wait to see these recommendations impact the experience of 90+ million riders, and I couldn’t be more thankful to my manager and mentor for giving me the opportunity, support, and sense of agency.

Project 2 Overview

I took the momentum from Project 1 into Project 2, as I continued to work on the trip experience, just on a different phase. For my second project, I conducted a diary study on dScout with 21 participants to capture their perceptions of advertisements while on the move. Throughout the study week, I organized 430+ photo and video entries, which culminated into 3 high-level insights and 9 attitudinal patterns. Before wrapping up my internship, I presented these findings by outlining a framework of 12+ tenets and traps to inform the Sales, Product, Brand, and Ads teams about rider discovery opportunities.

In hindsight, I’m most proud of how I aligned visions from different stakeholders for this area of work. At one point, I introduced the TripEx team to a related field of work at Uber that they hadn’t been aware of beforehand; along the same vein, I looped in managers from different circles of the company throughout my project to learn from their insightful perspectives. Altogether, the people and projects that I had the pleasure to work with during this internship was truly a dream come true in my life as a UX researcher.

Key Takeaways

Be a research partner, not a research resource

These are some of the many wise words from my manager, Kendall Avery. I have come across this lesson all throughout the different phases of a project, from scoping to share-out. First, your stakeholders will have many asks, and just as many visions; in the end, however, they appreciate your expertise, and your judgment around how grand of a project can be supported. Once you’re up and running, it is also your responsibility to delegate work to your team; it’s a two-way street between you and your stakeholders. Along this same vein, don’t just answer questions when you share your recommendations; ask them! How might the team move forward with these insights in mind?

Inspiration finds you, but only when you’re working

I came across this feeling all throughout my internship. On a day-to-day basis, doing your homework might mean coming to each meeting with a yes-or-no question to ask. From project to project, inspiration came immediately during my background lit review, where elements of past study designs lived on to inspire my own. In the office itself, hard work included energizing my friends in the Paris section of Floor 4, Mission Bay 3; this energy reflected back onto me, as these friends became idols of mine from their love, support, and help introducing me to new stakeholders.

Altogether, hard work in a fast environment champions running with inspiration, and looking back, it’s cool to think of the different directions that the project took thanks to little moments in the day. Moving forward, I’m confident that whatever direction my career path takes, I’ll enjoy the ride as long as I continue doing what I love.

Listening helps sharpen your toolkit

As an extrovert, I saw a growth opportunity in learning to listen more. On the surface, this is most obvious during research sessions. After all, at a place with so many research resources, operations, and bright minds, this help is for one thing: to preserve your energy for Uber riders themselves. Nevertheless, I took advantage of my listening skills at other points in the research process as well. First, I found inspiration from how a PM frames a problem; adopting one framework from a meeting, I organized all of my background research sources using a What – So What – Now What – Woah! framework. Furthermore, during study design, I made sure to let design fuel UXR, empowering my designers’ idea for a photo-based storyboard behind an interactive prototype.

One takeaway to prioritize listening is to share early and often; there’s never a bad time to ask for feedback, and the more you do, the more effectively you’ll work in small chunks to crack at a big problem. Moving forward, I’m excited to continue listening to my peers and idols, and I can’t thank Uber enough for all of the learnings I’ve gained from them, meeting by meeting.
